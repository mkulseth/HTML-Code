Project Overview

Many DoD facilities throughout the eastern United States utilize low intensity prescribed fire to manage hazardous fuels, restore ecological characteristics and historic fire regimes, and encourage the recovery of threatened and endangered species in the forests they manage. Current predictive models used to simulate fire behavior during low-intensity prescribed fires (and wildfires) are empirically-based, simplistic, and often fail to adequately capture variability in fuel characteristics and interactions with important meteorological processes. The SERDP fire project utilizes a suite of measurements at the fuel particle, fuel bed, field plot, and stand scales to quantify how variability in fuel characteristics and key meteorological factors interact to drive fire behavior during low intensity prescribed burns. These experiments are designed to provide information for the further development and evaluation of mechanistic, physics-based models that explicitly account for combustion, turbulent transfer, and energy exchange by coupling and scaling individual component processes. These will improve our understanding of and ability to accurately predict fire behavior under a wide range of management scenarios.

Objectives

  1. Improve our understanding of the processes driving heat transfer, ignition, thermal degradation, flaming and smoldering combustion, mass consumption, and fire propagation at the scale of individual fuel particles and fuel layers in low-intensity surface fires
  2. Develop an understanding of how fuel consumption is affected by spatial variability in fuel particle type, fuel moisture status, bulk density, and horizontal and vertical arrangement of fuel components in low-intensity surface fires
  3. Increase our understanding of the effects of multi-scale atmospheric dynamics, including ambient and fire- and forest overstory-induced turbulence, on fire spread and convective heat transfer in low-intensity surface fires
  4. Ensure that the measurements undertaken support the development and validation of physics-based fire behavior models using an iterative approach consisting of laboratory, field, and model simulations


The work conducted by our team at MSU, in collaboration with several other scientists on the SERDP fire project, will help address objectives 3 and 4. Specifically, we will:

  • Analyze the data collected from the fuel-bed scale experiments to further the understanding of how prescribed fires at the fuel-bed scale modify the mean and turbulent flows in the surface layer
  • Conduct pre-experiment model simulations using the ARPS-CANOPY model to understand conditions in the atmospheric boundary layer at the potential burn sites in support of the planning of the management scale field experiments
  • Carry out post-experiment simulations of the management-scale field experiment to help explain the observations and elucidate fire-atmosphere interactions
